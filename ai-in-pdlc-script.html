Overall Objective

The purpose of this work is to demonstrate how AI can be applied throughout the entire Product Development Lifecycle — from discovery through strategy, design, delivery, testing and analytics — and to show how the role of Product Manager continues to evolve as AI becomes embedded in delivery practices.

This is not a technology demo. Every AI capability shown connects back to a product decision, a user outcome, or a business implication. The central question is not "what can AI do?" but "how do Product Managers create value in an AI-native delivery environment?"

Objective of This Demo

Objective of This Demo

Primary goal: demonstrate how AI can be leveraged throughout the PDLC and show the evolving PM role at every stage. The NHS patient feedback analysis platform is the example use case — chosen because it is a realistic, high-value business problem where AI adds clear and demonstrable value.

The underlying challenge (processing large volumes of qualitative feedback to surface risks and insights) generalises across healthcare, telecoms, banking, and public sector.

What AI in PDLC Means

AI in PDLC is not a single tool or a coding assistant. It means AI is embedded as a continuous co-worker at every phase:

  • Discovery: synthesising research, building personas, generating strategy artefacts
  • Design: prototyping, assumption testing, persona simulation, iterative refinement
  • Delivery: repo setup, CI/CD, prompt review, code generation, documentation
  • Testing: persona-based user testing, accessibility, behavioural coverage
  • Operations: embedded intelligence, insight generation, anomaly detection, executive analytics
How to Achieve It

Three enablers, implemented as sequential steps:

  1. Load all discovery artefacts (personas, PRD, OKRs, roadmap, strategy) into an AI instance that acts as a product co-worker — context stays available throughout the lifecycle.
  2. Use AI to challenge assumptions before real users see anything — persona simulation, journey testing, design critique.
  3. Embed AI into the product itself, not just the team's workflow — surface priority items, generate drafts, provide natural language analytics for executives.
Evolving Role of PM

Two paths are worth exploring:

  • New specialist role: AI workflow orchestrator / orchestration lead — someone who coordinates AI tools, agents and outputs across an organisation.
  • Evolution of existing PM role: PMs expand into AI economics, workflow orchestration, tool selection strategy, and total cost of ownership management.
Organisational context
In startups and scale-ups, PMs already own build vs buy decisions, budgets, and TCO. In large enterprises (HMRC, NHS), architecture and governance teams own many of these — PMs influence through expertise and evidence. This presentation acknowledges both contexts.

Product Ops

AI contributions:

  • Embedded intelligence: the dashboard surfaces priority items, identifies critical cases, explains why they matter, suggests next actions — users don't navigate raw data
  • Insight-to-action flow: user sees case → AI generates response draft → user reviews and acts — reducing administration time
  • AI-powered analytics: rather than showing charts, the system explains whether objectives are being achieved, whether performance is improving, whether risks are emerging — natural language intelligence above traditional dashboards
  • Anomaly detection: AI flags deteriorating wards before they appear in operational metrics
  • Persona-based testing in production: AI assumes user persona identities to generate ongoing behavioural test coverage
PM role: define the intelligence layer (what should be surfaced, in what format, at what threshold), monitor AI cost per operation, evaluate model performance, own the TCO of running the AI product.

  • Prototype generation: design intent → working prototype in one session
  • Assumption testing: AI challenges the design before users see it, raising quality

  • Service blueprint creation: auto-generated from artefacts, then refined — workshop effort reduced significantly

  • Documentation as a release gate: DECISIONS.md enforced by CI — stays current automatically
  • Overall: 3–4 engineering sprints → 1 sprint for a full production-quality stack
Product Management
  • Strategy artefacts: market research, OKRs, roadmap — generated from discovery evidence, not blank-page effort
  • Discovery synthesis: personas, pain points, opportunities — built from evidence, not assumptions
  • Scope decisions accelerated: AI helps evaluate each hypothesis against cost, user value, and strategic fit simultaneously
  • TCO visibility: AI gives PMs visibility of architectural cost implications earlier — before decisions are locked in

D

Final Takeaway & Next Steps

The central message

AI can support every phase of the Product Development Lifecycle — from discovery through strategy, design, delivery, testing and analytics. But AI does not remove PM accountability. It expands it.

Product Managers who understand AI economics, workflow orchestration, and total cost of ownership will be better positioned to build products that are not just valuable but sustainably profitable at scale.

What PMs need to do
  1. Own the AI economics conversation — understand cost per query, agent execution costs, model selection trade-offs, and total cost of ownership. Ask: "What is the total cost of running this product, and how can we reduce it without reducing value?"
  2. Get involved in technical discovery — architectural decisions made at this stage lock in TCO for years. PMs who wait until delivery miss the moment when costs are determined.
  3. Use AI to accelerate discovery, not skip it — AI can generate personas, research, OKRs and strategy artefacts faster, but they still need PM validation, curation, and connection to real user evidence.
  4. Treat AI outputs as starting points, not finished products — AI drafts are high quality but require PM judgement to validate, prioritise, and connect to business outcomes.
  5. Build with AI embedded in the product, not just the team — the most durable value comes from embedding AI intelligence into the user experience itself: surfacing insights, drafting responses, explaining metrics, detecting risks.
  6. Advocate for lean AI architectures — fewer tokens, cleaner code, leaner workflows. Small efficiencies at development time compound into significant operational savings.
  7. Evolve your role proactively — whether in a startup or a large enterprise, understand AI workflow orchestration enough to influence decisions, even if you don't own them directly.
The NHS platform as a reusable framework

The platform built for this demo is not a one-off. It is the beginning of a reusable framework for demonstrating AI value across discovery, design, delivery and operations. As new use cases are added (telecoms, banking, public sector), the framework becomes richer.

The same fundamental capability — processing large volumes of qualitative feedback, surfacing risks, generating insight — applies across any organisation that interacts directly with customers or citizens at scale.

The Problem Statement & Why We Chose It

The Problem

NHS trusts face growing financial and reputational exposure from patient feedback they cannot analyse at scale. The 10 Year Health Plan (July 2025) directly ties trust income to patient ratings through clinical team payments, patient power payments, and publicly published league tables updated quarterly from summer 2025. Yet most trusts still rely on manual coding, sampled data, and disconnected reporting systems that prevent timely action. There is no NHS-native platform that combines AI-powered theme classification, closed-loop feedback management, and integration with clinical systems — leaving organisations unable to detect emerging risks early or demonstrate improvement to regulators and boards.

Why this problem — and why now

Three forces converged to make this the right problem to solve at this moment:

Force 1 · Financial materiality just changed
The 10 Year Health Plan (July 2025) made patient feedback financially consequential for the first time. Trust income is now directly linked to patient ratings via three mechanisms: clinical team payments, patient power payments, and public quarterly league tables. A poor feedback quarter has a direct, quantifiable income consequence. This moved patient experience from a compliance checkbox to a board-level financial risk.
Force 2 · Current tools are structurally broken
The dominant "solution" is a Quality Manager spending 2–3 days every quarter manually coding NHS Friends & Family Test (FFT) free-text in Excel — on a 10–20% sample. That means 80–90% of patient feedback is never read. Issues surface weeks or months after they occur, from complaints or press coverage rather than from data. No existing NHS-native platform combines AI theme classification, risk alerting, and closed-loop action management.
Force 3 · An 18–24 month window before the market closes
311 NHS trusts represent an £18–40M ARR addressable market. The Medium Term Planning Framework (October 2025) already mandates near-real-time feedback capture on at least 5 wards before discharge — a current compliance requirement. US competitors (NRC Health, Qualtrics) have not yet made a serious NHS push. The combination of regulatory pressure, unmet need, and absent NHS-native competition creates a rare entry window.
Who bears this problem

Three distinct personas are directly affected — each experiencing the same data gap differently:

PersonaCore painWhat they need from an MVP
Chief Nurse / Director of Patient Experience Finds out about ward problems from press or complaints — never from data. Board packs take 2–3 days to compile manually per quarter. 24-hour risk alerts when a ward crosses a threshold. Auto-generated board pack (PDF). One-click CQC evidence export.
Quality Manager / Patient Experience Lead Manually codes 10–20% of FFT free-text. Real issues go undetected. Cannot prove to the board that feedback led to measurable improvement. AI theme classification on 100% of comments. Top-3 deteriorating wards dashboard. 3-click drilldown: alert → theme → verbatim.
Ward Manager / Service Line Lead Feedback arrives 4–6 weeks after a patient's stay. Aggregated scores say nothing specific. Cannot engage with another system during a 12-hour shift. One weekly digest email (Monday 08:00). Top 2 concerns + top 2 positives + 1 verbatim. No login required. One-tap share with team.
Why this problem is the right scope for discovery
Scope rationale
The problem is large enough to be commercially meaningful (£18–40M ARR, 311 trusts) but narrow enough to be solvable with a focused MVP. The three personas have clearly differentiated needs that map to three distinct product surfaces: an executive dashboard, an analyst workflow, and a ward-facing digest. This clean segmentation made it possible to define MVP scope without endless stakeholder negotiation — each persona gets one core outcome, not everything at once.
AI's role in refining the problem statement
AI synthesised the problem statement from raw interview notes, NHS policy documents (10YHP, MTPF), FFT data analysis, and competitive research. It identified the three-force structure (financial materiality, broken tools, market window) as the clearest way to communicate urgency to both clinical and commercial audiences. The final statement was iterated with the team until it could be read cold by a board member and immediately understood.

AI Across the Five PDLC Stages

The project's discovery workflow was structured around five sequential stages. AI accelerated each stage in a distinct way.

1
User Interviews

AI assisted in synthesising raw interview notes into structured personas. The three seed personas in the app were shaped by AI-driven thematic analysis — identifying common pain points, motivations, and contexts across interviewees. Optional video URL fields on each persona allow attaching actual interview recordings, keeping AI-generated summaries anchored to source evidence.

Efficiency gains
Manual persona synthesis from multiple interviews typically takes 2–3 days of a researcher's time — identifying themes, clustering pain points, writing up structured outputs. AI reduced this to a single session: transcripts in, structured personas out, with video evidence attached for traceability. The team spent their time reviewing and refining rather than building from scratch.
2–3 days → hours 3 personas synthesised in one session 100% of interview notes processed Evidence-linked via video URL fields
PM cost lens — what a future PM tracks here
User interview synthesis is typically one of the most token-intensive discovery activities. A PM accountable for AI economics asks: how many tokens did it cost to process raw interview transcripts into structured personas? Could a smaller, fine-tuned model do this more cheaply? Could interview guides be designed upfront to reduce the volume of unstructured text the AI needs to process? At this stage, the PM is already thinking about cost-to-insight, not just insight quality.
2
Feedback Analysis

AI was used to ingest and parse NHS Friends & Family Test (FFT) data. The client-side Excel/CSV parser (using the xlsx library) combined with AI-assisted deduplication logic allowed rapid triage of patient feedback — categorising responses and surfacing patterns without a backend. This directly informed the MVP problem statement.

Efficiency gains
The existing NHS process coded 10–20% of FFT free-text manually over 2–3 days per quarter. AI-assisted analysis covered 100% of comments, identified themes, and surfaced patterns in a fraction of the time — and without any server infrastructure. Deduplication logic that would have required custom backend development was generated and tested in a single session.
10–20% sample → 100% coverage 2–3 days manual → hours automated Zero backend infrastructure Dedup logic generated & tested in one session
PM cost lens — what a future PM tracks here
Running LLM theme classification across 100% of FFT comments (rather than a 10–20% sample) is the core product value — but it is also a recurring cost driver. A PM owns the question: what is the cost per 1,000 comments classified? Can we batch classify overnight rather than in real-time to use lower-cost inference tiers? Can we reduce token consumption by preprocessing comments (stripping boilerplate, normalising encoding) before they reach the model? The decision to parse client-side (using xlsx with no backend) was also a cost decision: it eliminated server-side compute entirely for the ingest step.
3
Technical Discovery

AI was used to generate the Technical Discovery Report — a seeded artefact covering NHS infrastructure constraints (HSCN, N3, network proxies, data residency, identity/access), MVP architecture proposals, and reusable component patterns. This gave the team a structured starting point for technical scoping without weeks of manual research.

Efficiency gains
A traditional technical discovery scoping NHS infrastructure constraints — HSCN, N3, data residency, proxy environments, identity/access — typically takes 2–4 weeks of an architect's time. AI generated a structured, detailed Technical Discovery Report covering all of these areas in a single session, ready for team review and iteration. The report was seeded directly into the app as an artefact, visible to all stakeholders immediately.
2–4 weeks → 1 session NHS infra constraints fully documented MVP architecture proposed & structured Report auto-seeded to all users
PM cost lens — what a future PM tracks here
Technical discovery is where architectural decisions lock in the total cost of ownership for years. A forward-deployed PM sits in this phase and asks: if we choose this architecture, what are the ongoing agent-processing costs? NHS infrastructure constraints (HSCN, data residency, proxy environments) affect which cloud services and AI APIs can be called — and therefore which cost optimisations are available. A PM who only reviews features at this stage misses the moment when TCO is determined. The choice of Firebase over a custom backend, for example, eliminated an entire class of server-side compute costs.
4
Ideation & Hypotheses

AI helped the team move from raw discovery findings to structured OKRs and a prioritised roadmap. The Artefacts tab stores all discovery outputs — OKRs, research plan, findings — as rich HTML content, allowing AI-drafted content to be iterated and refined collaboratively in real time.

Efficiency gains
Translating raw discovery findings into OKRs, a research plan, and a prioritised roadmap typically requires multiple facilitated workshops and several rounds of written synthesis. AI produced first-draft versions of all three outputs from discovery inputs, collapsing what would normally be 1–2 weeks of facilitation and write-up into an afternoon. The team's role shifted from drafting to curating — a fundamentally faster loop.
1–2 weeks → afternoon OKRs, roadmap & research plan drafted in one pass Team curates rather than drafts All outputs live-editable & Firebase-persisted
PM cost lens — what a future PM tracks here
Ideation is where scope decisions multiply future costs. Every hypothesis that makes it into the roadmap becomes a feature that needs AI workflows to support it. A PM with cost accountability evaluates each hypothesis not just on user value, but on its agent economics: does this feature require a real-time LLM call on every user action, or can it be run asynchronously? Does it need a large model, or would a smaller, cheaper model be sufficient? Removing the Ingest tab from MVP scope (DD-06) was exactly this kind of decision — it eliminated a recurring cost with no demo value, freeing budget for higher-value AI workflows.
5
Design Prototypes

AI generated the React + Vite frontend, the Figma-to-code component pipeline (via Figma MCP), and the UI design system — Playfair Display for authority, DM Sans for clarity, JetBrains Mono for metadata — directly from design intent. This collapsed what would normally be a multi-sprint frontend build into a single discovery sprint.

Efficiency gains
Building a production-quality React prototype with Firebase sync, drag-and-drop Kanban, rich text editing, an acceptance test suite, and a full CI/CD pipeline would typically take 3–4 engineering sprints. AI delivered the entire stack — components, hooks, seed data, Playwright tests, CI pipeline, and deployment config — in a single discovery sprint. The Figma MCP integration further eliminated the design-to-code translation step entirely.
3–4 sprints → 1 sprint Full React stack generated end-to-end CI/CD + Playwright tests included Figma → code with zero manual translation Production deployed on Vercel
PM cost lens — what a future PM tracks here
This is the compounding efficiency argument in practice. AI-generated code that is leaner and more consistent produces less code overall — less code to review, less code passing through CI/CD pipelines, fewer lines analysed by AI code-review agents, and therefore lower ongoing agent-processing costs. A PM who selects tools that generate less code is not just reducing immediate development time — they are reducing the total volume of AI-assisted review, testing, and maintenance work that will be incurred every sprint for the life of the product. Small tooling choices at prototype stage compound into significant cost differences at scale.

Decision-by-Decision: Why & What It Means

Each architectural, design, and coding decision was made intentionally. Here is the reasoning and the product consequence for each.

AD-01 Firebase Realtime Database as the persistence layer

All app state is persisted to Firebase Realtime Database — not Firestore, not a REST API, not localStorage.

Why this decision
Realtime sync via onValue listeners required zero backend infrastructure. The app's flat key-value data shape matched Firebase's model perfectly. Firestore's querying power would have been wasted overhead.
Product implication
Any team member opening the app sees live updates within milliseconds. This made the tool genuinely collaborative during workshop sessions — a presenter could update PDLC cards and all participants would see changes instantly. The trade-off: no per-user isolation means everyone shares the same data, which is intentional for a single-team tool but would need revisiting at scale.
AI role
AI generated the useFirebaseSync hook, the debouncing logic, and the JSON serialisation strategy — turning a complex real-time sync problem into a reusable, tested abstraction in hours.
AD-02 Store all Firebase values as JSON strings

Every value written to Firebase is JSON.stringify-d on write and JSON.parse-d on read.

Why this decision
Firebase silently mutates arrays — it strips holes and converts numeric-keyed arrays to objects. Serialising to strings guarantees exact round-trips for nested arrays, typed fields, and complex structures.
Product implication
Users can reorder Kanban cards, edit persona bullet lists, and update PDLC phase descriptions without their work being silently corrupted by Firebase's type coercion. This was a discovered-in-production bug that AI helped diagnose and resolve systematically.
AD-03 Seed-if-empty — never overwrite user data

Default content only writes to Firebase if a path does not already exist. SEED_VERSION signals "check for new empty paths," not "reset everything."

Why this decision
The original implementation used unconditional set() — every version bump reverted all in-app edits. The fix (seed-if-empty + merge-by-ID for arrays) was discovered after real user data was lost.
Product implication
Teams can safely workshop content in the app across multiple sessions. Updating the app to add new artefacts does not wipe existing work. This was critical for maintaining credibility during live stakeholder demos.
AI role
AI identified the root cause of the overwrite bug and generated the merge-by-ID strategy for marketResearch arrays — a non-trivial upsert pattern implemented correctly on the first attempt.
AD-04 Debounced writes + local pending flag

Local state updates are immediate. Firebase writes are debounced 800ms. A localPendingRef suppresses remote echoes while a write is in flight.

Why this decision
Without debouncing, every keystroke in an editable field triggers a Firebase write. Without the pending flag, Firebase echoes the write back to the originating client, overwriting in-progress input.
Product implication
Editing feels instant and local. Users experience zero lag while typing, yet all changes are durably saved. The flush() method on save buttons ensures nothing is lost on tab switches.
AD-05 No user authentication

No login. All users share full read/write access to the same Firebase database.

Why this decision
Auth adds friction and complexity with no benefit for a single internal discovery team. Speed of iteration mattered more than access control at this stage.
Product implication
Any team member can open the URL and start contributing immediately — no onboarding barrier. The known risk (anyone with the URL can edit everything) is acceptable for current scope. If the tool is opened to wider NHS stakeholders, Firebase Auth would be the natural next step.
AD-06 Secrets in GitHub Actions — never committed

All tokens (Vercel, Atlassian, Figma) live in GitHub Secrets or local .env files. Nothing is committed to the repo.

Why this decision
A Figma token (figd_...) and Atlassian token were accidentally committed to README.md. GitHub's secret scanning blocked the push entirely. Both tokens were revoked and removed — the incident enforced the policy.
Product implication
The CI/CD pipeline can be shared, forked, or handed over to another team without risk of credential exposure. The incident response (revoke → remove → allow-list → policy) is documented so the team doesn't repeat it.
AD-07 develop → main promotion gate

Production deploys only happen through npm run promote, which enforces: build passes → local review at localhost:5173 → merge → CI deploy.

Why this decision
Playwright tests catch functional regressions, but not visual or UX issues. The local review step catches problems that automated tests miss.
Product implication
Stakeholders always see a reviewed, tested version of the product. No half-finished feature or broken layout has ever reached the production demo URL. The promote script makes the review step unavoidable — it's enforced, not aspirational.
AD-08 Client-side password gate (session-only)

A PasswordGate component wraps the app. Password stored in sessionStorage — gate re-appears on tab close.

Why this decision
Keeps casual visitors out of the demo without Firebase Auth overhead. The password is intentionally in the JS bundle — accepted for a low-stakes internal demo.
Product implication
The production URL can be shared freely with the team without risk of anonymous public edits. The session-only storage means each browser tab session gets a fresh gate — users can't accidentally stay authenticated across days.
DD-01 Static config / editable data split in PDLC

Icons and colours are static (PHASE_CONFIG); titles, descriptions, bullets are editable and persisted (EditablePhase).

Why this decision
React nodes (icons) can't be serialised to Firebase. Design constants (gradients, colours) shouldn't be user-editable — they represent the product's visual identity.
Product implication
Teams can fully customise PDLC content without breaking the visual design. The app stays on-brand regardless of what text a user enters. AI-generated the initial PDLC content structure and default phase text.
DD-02 Flip card progressive disclosure for PDLC activities

Card front: title + tag. Card back: description + bullets. "Edit & view all" opens a full modal.

Why this decision
The PDLC grid is used as a presentation artefact. Keeping the default view scannable prevents information overload during demos.
Product implication
During stakeholder demos, the presenter can walk through the PDLC at a high level and drill into any card on demand. The interaction pattern mirrors how the product team thinks — overview first, detail on request.
DD-03 Discovery cards ordered by execution sequence

User Interviews → Feedback Analysis → Tech Discovery → Ideation → Design Prototypes — in the order the team actually executes them.

Why this decision
The PDLC section is both documentation and a presentation tool. Execution order makes it readable as a plan, not just a list.
Product implication
New team members can read the PDLC section and immediately understand the discovery sequence. Stakeholders can follow the narrative from left to right during demos.
DD-06 Ingest tab removed from MVP demo scope

The NHS FFT Data Ingest tab and all related components were removed. Default tab is now User Analysis.

Why this decision
The Ingest tab was an internal utility, not part of the demo narrative. Including it distracted from the core story: personas, discovery artefacts, PDLC, and delivery backlog.
Product implication
Demos are tighter and more focused. The app's default state opens on User Analysis — the most compelling starting point for stakeholders who care about patient outcomes. This is a classic product decision: what you remove matters as much as what you keep.
CD-09 DECISIONS.md as a release gate

CI fails if DECISIONS.md is missing, structurally invalid, or out of date relative to changed architectural files.

Why this decision
Documentation that isn't enforced becomes stale within weeks. Tying it to the release gate makes the friction intentional — the build breaks, or the doc gets updated.
Product implication
Any future maintainer — or AI agent — can read DECISIONS.md and understand exactly why the system is the way it is. This reduces onboarding time and prevents "why is this here?" archaeology through git history.
AI role
The CI check, the Playwright static test, and the DECISIONS.md content itself were all generated by AI — creating a self-reinforcing documentation loop where AI writes decisions and then verifies they are kept current.

Key Takeaways

AI compressed a multi-sprint build into a single discovery sprint The React frontend, Firebase sync layer, Playwright test suite, CI pipeline, and all seed content were AI-generated. What would typically take 3–4 sprints of engineering was completed in one, freeing the team to focus on discovery outcomes rather than implementation.
Decisions were made to serve the demo narrative, not engineering ideals No auth, client-side password, shared database, ingest tab removed — every "shortcut" was a deliberate product choice to keep the demo focused and accessible. The trade-offs are documented explicitly so they can be revisited when the tool scales.

The Evolving PM Role in AI-Native Delivery

In the final part of the discussion with Umang, the question shifted from what AI can do in the PDLC to what the PM role becomes when AI is embedded throughout it. The conclusion: the PM accountability surface needs to expand significantly.

The core argument

Product Managers should become accountable not just for product outcomes, but for the total cost of developing, operating, and maintaining AI-powered products. The traditional PM scope — features, user value, delivery prioritisation — is necessary but no longer sufficient.

The PM's question shifts from "what should we build next?" to:

"What is the total cost of running this product, and how can we reduce it without reducing value?"
What the expanded PM cost visibility covers
LLM query costs

Cost per inference call, model selection, token consumption per workflow — tracked per feature, not just in aggregate.

Agent orchestration costs

Cost of chaining agents, retry logic, tool calls, and intermediate steps — often the hidden cost in multi-agent workflows.

Discovery & delivery activity costs

AI-assisted research, synthesis, code generation, review — the cost of building the product, not just running it.

Ongoing maintenance costs

Prompt updates, model migrations, fine-tuning, anomaly investigation — the invisible recurring spend after launch.

Failure & anomaly costs

Cost of hallucinations, misclassifications, agent failures — including the human time to detect, triage, and fix them.

Product lifecycle economics

Total cost of ownership from first commit to decommission — including the compounding costs of technical and AI debt.

The compounding efficiency argument — illustrated

Small tooling choices at development time compound into significant cost differences during operation. This was illustrated with a concrete example:

Example · Tool selection → token reduction → downstream cost chain

A tool that generates less code creates a chain of savings:

Code written
Code reviewed (human)
CI/CD pipeline runs
AI code-review agent cost
Ongoing maintenance load

Each bar represents the downstream cost impact of the original tooling choice. A PM who selects a leaner code-generation tool at prototype stage is compressing every bar in this chain — across every sprint, for the entire product lifetime.

The Forward Deployed PM — building on Umang's framing

This builds on Umang's earlier concept of Forward Deployed Product Managers / Engineers — PMs who sit at the intersection of product, technology, and business. The cost accountability dimension adds a fourth lens:

Traditional PM scope

Product strategy · Customer value · Delivery prioritisation · Stakeholder alignment

AI-native PM scope (added)

AI workflow orchestration · Tool selection economics · Agent cost modelling · Total cost of ownership management · Cost-to-serve optimisation

What this looked like on this project

Several decisions in this project already reflected this expanded accountability — even if not framed that way at the time:

Strategic takeaway The emerging PM role in AI-native organisations is not a narrower specialisation — it is a broader one. PMs who can hold product strategy, customer value, delivery prioritisation, and AI workflow economics simultaneously will be the ones who build products that are not just valuable, but sustainably profitable at scale.
